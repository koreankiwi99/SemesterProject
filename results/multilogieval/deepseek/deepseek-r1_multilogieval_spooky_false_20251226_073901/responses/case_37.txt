The goal is to evaluate the statement: "If Jake cannot relax outside, is he prepared?" by translating the context into Lean and attempting to prove the negation of the conditional (i.e., that Jake cannot relax outside and is not prepared). If successful, the answer is "No"; otherwise, "Failure".

### Premises Translation:
1. **If the weather was nice, then they could relax outside.**  
   `WeatherNice → RelaxOutside`
2. **If they read books, that means they packed enough.**  
   `ReadBooks → PackedEnough`
3. **Either the weather was nice, or they did not pack enough, or both.**  
   This simplifies to `WeatherNice ∨ ¬PackedEnough` (as the disjunction covers all cases).
4. **If someone is prepared, they read books.**  
   `Prepared → ReadBooks`

Jake is the person in context, so all predicates apply to him.

### Attempt to Prove Negation:
The statement to evaluate is: "If Jake cannot relax outside, is he prepared?"  
- This corresponds to the conditional: `¬RelaxOutside → Prepared`  
- Its negation is: `¬(¬RelaxOutside → Prepared)`, which is equivalent to `¬RelaxOutside ∧ ¬Prepared`  

We attempt to prove `¬RelaxOutside ∧ ¬Prepared` from the premises.

### Lean Analysis:

axiom obj : Type
axiom Jake : obj
axiom WeatherNice : Prop
axiom RelaxOutside : Prop
axiom ReadBooks : Prop
axiom PackedEnough : Prop
axiom Prepared : Prop

axiom P2 : ReadBooks → PackedEnough
axiom P3 : WeatherNice ∨ ¬PackedEnough
axiom P4 : Prepared → ReadBooks

theorem negate_conditional : ¬RelaxOutside ∧ ¬Prepared := by
  -- To prove ¬RelaxOutside ∧ ¬Prepared, we need both ¬RelaxOutside and ¬Prepared.
  -- However, the premises do not provide evidence for ¬RelaxOutside.
  -- We can derive implications but not the required conjunction.
  have aux : ¬RelaxOutside → ¬Prepared := by
    intro nr
    have nw : ¬WeatherNice := fun w => nr (P1 w)
    cases P3 with
    | inl w => exact False.elim (nw w)
    | inr np => 
        have nread : ¬ReadBooks := fun r => np (P2 r)
        exact fun p => nread (P4 p)
  -- We now have: ¬RelaxOutside → ¬Prepared, but we lack ¬RelaxOutside.
  -- Without ¬RelaxOutside, we cannot construct ¬Prepared or the conjunction.
  -- Classical reasoning (em) doesn't help since we cannot force ¬RelaxOutside.
  sorry  -- Proof fails
</lean>

### Explanation:

- **Derived implication**: From the premises, we prove `¬RelaxOutside → ¬Prepared` (if Jake cannot relax outside, he is not prepared). This uses:
  - Contrapositive of P1: `¬RelaxOutside → ¬WeatherNice`
  - P3: `¬WeatherNice` implies `¬PackedEnough` (from disjunction)
  - Contrapositives of P2 and P4: `¬PackedEnough → ¬ReadBooks → ¬Prepared`
- **Proof failure**: The premises do not provide `¬RelaxOutside` or `¬Prepared` as facts. The implication `¬RelaxOutside → ¬Prepared` holds, but its inverse `Prepared → RelaxOutside` (derivable from the premises) does not force `¬RelaxOutside`. Since `¬RelaxOutside ∧ ¬Prepared` cannot be proven, the negation of the conditional is unprovable.

### Conclusion:
The proof attempt fails. The answer is "Failure" because the premises do not entail the negation of the conditional.
